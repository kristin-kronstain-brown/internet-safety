## Ages 10–12: "People Can Manipulate Emotions Online"
Preteens begin social apps, group chats, fandoms, gaming communities, and independent browsing.

This is the right age to discuss:
- catfishing
- emotional manipulation
- misinformation
- social engineering
- reputation risks

### Define Important Terms
- Catfishing: When someone pretends to be a different person online to gain trust, attention, friendship, money, pictures, or emotional control.

    Examples:
    - pretending to be another kid
    - using stolen photos
    - lying about age or identity

    Teach:
    "You cannot fully verify someone just because they seem nice."

- Scam: A trick designed to get:
    - money
    - passwords
    - account access
    - personal information

- Social Engineering: A manipulation technique where someone uses psychology instead of hacking.

    Examples:
    - pretending to be support staff
    - pretending to be a friend
    - creating panic
    - guilt-tripping
    - pretending there’s an emergency

    Teach:
    "Many scams succeed because people are emotional, rushed, or trusting."

### Teach Emotional Manipulation
Kids this age need scripts they can recognize.

#### Common Manipulation Patterns
- Guilt: "If you were really my friend…"
- Isolation: "Nobody understands you except me."
- Pressure: "Send it now."
- Fake Emergencies: "Your account will be deleted!"
- Fear of Missing Out: "Everyone else already did it."

Teach kids to pause when emotions suddenly spike.

Good rule:
"Strong emotions are a signal to slow down."

#### App-Generic Privacy Habits
These work across almost all apps.

Teach kids to:
- keep accounts private when possible
- disable location sharing unless needed
- avoid posting live locations
- use nicknames instead of full names
- avoid showing school logos, addresses, schedules
- think before posting photos of bedrooms, cars, or neighborhoods
- decline friend requests from unknown people
- avoid quizzes collecting personal info

Explain why:
Small details can be combined into bigger profiles about a person.

Example:
A school shirt + first name + soccer field photo = enough information for someone to identify a child.
