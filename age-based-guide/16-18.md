## Ages 16–18: "You Are Managing Risk Like an Adult"

Older teens should learn:
- financial scams
- phishing
- employment scams
- identity theft
- data collection
- consent
- cybersecurity basics

### Teach Real-World Risk

Explain how information can be used:
- password reset questions
- identity verification
- targeted scams
- impersonation
- blackmail
- reputation attacks
- advertising profiles

Without fear:
"Most attacks use information people shared casually over time."

Phishing: Fake messages pretending to be trusted organizations.

  Examples:
  - fake bank alerts
  - fake shipping texts
  - fake school emails
  - fake login pages

  Teach:
  - don’t click rushed links
  - verify independently
  - check sender addresses carefully

### Teach Security Basics

Two-Factor Authentication

Explain:
Even if someone steals a password, they may still need a second code.

Encourage:
- authenticator apps
- unique passwords
- software updates
- recognizing suspicious login alerts

#### How to Teach Without Fear
Use Curiosity Instead of Threats

Avoid:
- "The internet is dangerous."
- "Everyone online is a predator."

Use:
- "Some people misuse information."
- "Good habits reduce problems."
- "You can learn to notice warning signs."

#### Normalize Asking for Help

Kids should know:
- they will not automatically lose device privileges
- they can report mistakes safely
- adults can help solve problems calmly

This is one of the biggest protective factors.

#### Practice Real Scenarios

Role-play:
- suspicious messages
- fake giveaways
- requests for secrets
- peer pressure
- someone asking for photos
- scam texts

Practice responses like:
- "I’m not comfortable with that."
- "I need to ask my parent."
- "I don’t share personal information online."
