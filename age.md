# Age-based guide to teaching kids about internet safety

Treat teaching your kids about internet safety like learning to cross a street or answer the door. Most people are fine, but kids need skills to notice problems, protect themselves, and ask for help.

The goal is not "stranger danger." Strangers can be helpful, too.

The goal is to build:
- confidence
- critical thinking
- emotional awareness
- healthy boundaries
- knowing when to pause and ask an adult

Kids learn best when:
- conversations happen regularly
- adults stay calm and nonjudgmental
- examples are realistic
- mistakes are treated as learning opportunities

## Ages 4–6: "My Information Belongs to Me"

At this age, focus on:
- privacy
- trusted adults
- asking before clicking or sharing
- understanding that people online are not automatically who they say they are

### Main Ideas
1. Personal Information

    Teach that some information is private:
    - full name
    - address
    - school
    - phone number
    - passwords
    - photos
    - where they are right now

    Simple phrase:
    "Private information stays with trusted grown-ups."

2. Ask Before You Tap

    Teach kids to:
    - ask before downloading
    - ask before replying
    - ask before sharing pictures
    - ask before clicking pop-ups

3. Online People Are Real People — But Sometimes Pretending

    Keep it simple:
    - "Some people online pretend to be someone else."
    - "Just like costumes at Halloween, people online can pretend."

4. Safe Feelings Rule

    Teach:
    - "If something online feels confusing, upsetting, secret, or pushy, tell a grown-up." This helps later with manipulation.


## Ages 7–9: "Not Everything Online Is True"

Kids this age start gaming, messaging, searching, and watching videos independently.

Focus on:
- manipulation
- tricks
- scams
- oversharing
- digital permanence

### Main Ideas
1. Introduce "Tricky Behavior"

    Some people online try to:
    - get attention
    - get information
    - get money
    - get pictures
    - get access to accounts
    
    Use examples:
    - "Click here to win!"
    - "Don’t tell your parents."
    - "You have to do this now."

    Teach kids:
    - "Safe adults don’t pressure kids into secrets."

2. Teach Basic Manipulation Tactics

    - Use kid-friendly language.
    - Flattery
    - "You’re so mature."
    - "You’re my favorite."

    Sometimes people say extra nice things to gain trust quickly.
    - Urgency
        - "Hurry!"
        - "You only have one chance!"

    Scammers want people to act before thinking.
    - Secrecy
    - "Keep this between us."
    
    Safe relationships do not depend on hiding things from trusted adults.

3. Explain "Digital Footprints"

    Anything shared online may:
    - be copied
    - be screenshot
    - spread to other people
    - Not as punishment — just reality.
    
    Good phrasing:
    "The internet remembers more than people expect."

4. Basic Password Safety

    Teach:
    - long passwords are stronger
    - don’t reuse passwords
    - don’t share passwords with friends
    - use parent-approved password managers when older
    
    Good rule:
    "Passwords are like toothbrushes: personal and not for sharing."

## Ages 10–12: "People Can Manipulate Emotions Online"
Preteens begin social apps, group chats, fandoms, gaming communities, and independent browsing.

This is the right age to discuss:
- catfishing
- emotional manipulation
- misinformation
- social engineering
- reputation risks

### Define Important Terms
- Catfishing: When someone pretends to be a different person online to gain trust, attention, friendship, money, pictures, or emotional control.

    Examples:
    - pretending to be another kid
    - using stolen photos
    - lying about age or identity

    Teach:
    "You cannot fully verify someone just because they seem nice."

- Scam: A trick designed to get:
    - money
    - passwords
    - account access
    - personal information

- Social Engineering: A manipulation technique where someone uses psychology instead of hacking.

    Examples:
    - pretending to be support staff
    - pretending to be a friend
    - creating panic
    - guilt-tripping
    - pretending there’s an emergency

    Teach:
    "Many scams succeed because people are emotional, rushed, or trusting."

### Teach Emotional Manipulation
Kids this age need scripts they can recognize.

#### Common Manipulation Patterns
- Guilt: "If you were really my friend…"
- Isolation: "Nobody understands you except me."
- Pressure: "Send it now."
- Fake Emergencies: "Your account will be deleted!"
- Fear of Missing Out: "Everyone else already did it."

Teach kids to pause when emotions suddenly spike.

Good rule:
"Strong emotions are a signal to slow down."

#### App-Generic Privacy Habits
These work across almost all apps.

Teach kids to:
- keep accounts private when possible
- disable location sharing unless needed
- avoid posting live locations
- use nicknames instead of full names
- avoid showing school logos, addresses, schedules
- think before posting photos of bedrooms, cars, or neighborhoods
- decline friend requests from unknown people
- avoid quizzes collecting personal info

Explain why:
Small details can be combined into bigger profiles about a person.

Example:
A school shirt + first name + soccer field photo = enough information for someone to identify a child.

## Ages 13–15: "Online Influence Is Designed to Affect You"

Teenagers need deeper discussions about:
- persuasion systems
- algorithms
- relationships
- sextortion
- misinformation
- privacy economics
- identity and reputation

Avoid lectures. Use collaboration.

### Explain How Platforms Influence Behavior

Teach that apps are often designed to:
- keep attention
- encourage sharing
- increase emotional reactions
- keep users scrolling

Not evil — just business design.

Teach teens to ask:
- Why is this being shown to me?
- What does this app gain if I stay longer?
- Is this trying to make me emotional?

### Teach Advanced Manipulation

Love Bombing: Excessive affection or attention used to gain fast emotional dependence.
    Signs:
    - instant intense attachment
    - pressure for constant contact
    - guilt for setting boundaries

Sextortion: When someone threatens to share images or information unless demands are met.
    Important teaching:
    - victims are often manipulated gradually
    - shame keeps people silent
    - getting help early matters
    -  Avoid blame-focused language.

### Reputation and Permanence

Discuss:
- screenshots
- colleges/employers
- impersonation
- account breaches
- AI-generated fake images/videos

Teach:
"Your online identity is partly built by what you post and partly by what others can collect."

### Healthy Skepticism Skills

Teach teens to verify:
- images
- viral claims
- "news"
- emotional posts
- giveaways
- investment schemes

Ask:
- Who benefits?
- Is there evidence?
- Is this trying to provoke outrage?
- Can I verify this elsewhere?

## Ages 16–18: "You Are Managing Risk Like an Adult"

Older teens should learn:
- financial scams
- phishing
- employment scams
- identity theft
- data collection
- consent
- cybersecurity basics

### Teach Real-World Risk

Explain how information can be used:
- password reset questions
- identity verification
- targeted scams
- impersonation
- blackmail
- reputation attacks
- advertising profiles

Without fear:
"Most attacks use information people shared casually over time."

Phishing: Fake messages pretending to be trusted organizations.

  Examples:
  - fake bank alerts
  - fake shipping texts
  - fake school emails
  - fake login pages

  Teach:
  - don’t click rushed links
  - verify independently
  - check sender addresses carefully

### Teach Security Basics

Two-Factor Authentication

Explain:
Even if someone steals a password, they may still need a second code.

Encourage:
- authenticator apps
- unique passwords
- software updates
- recognizing suspicious login alerts

#### How to Teach Without Fear
Use Curiosity Instead of Threats

Avoid:
- "The internet is dangerous."
- "Everyone online is a predator."

Use:
- "Some people misuse information."
- "Good habits reduce problems."
- "You can learn to notice warning signs."

#### Normalize Asking for Help

Kids should know:
- they will not automatically lose device privileges
- they can report mistakes safely
- adults can help solve problems calmly

This is one of the biggest protective factors.

#### Practice Real Scenarios

Role-play:
- suspicious messages
- fake giveaways
- requests for secrets
- peer pressure
- someone asking for photos
- scam texts

Practice responses like:
- "I’m not comfortable with that."
- "I need to ask my parent."
- "I don’t share personal information online."

## A Simple Family Framework

You can teach almost everything through five questions:
- Who is asking?
- Why do they want this?
- How does this make me feel?
- What could happen if I share this?
- Would I be okay if this became public?

That framework scales from kindergarten through adulthood.
